ACCEPTANCE AND COMMITMENT THERAPY (ACT) — CORE KNOWLEDGE BASE

=== WHAT IS ACT ===
Acceptance and Commitment Therapy focuses on psychological flexibility — the ability to be present, open to experience, and to act in line with personal values. Unlike CBT which challenges thoughts, ACT teaches you to change your relationship with thoughts rather than their content.

=== SIX CORE PROCESSES ===

1. ACCEPTANCE
Allow thoughts and feelings to exist without fighting them.
"I notice I am having the thought that I am a failure" — this creates distance.
Fighting anxiety makes it stronger. Acceptance paradoxically reduces suffering.

2. COGNITIVE DEFUSION
Unhook from thoughts — see them as mental events, not facts.
Techniques:
- "I'm having the thought that..." prefix
- Sing the thought to a silly tune
- Say the thought very slowly
- Thank your mind: "Thanks mind, that's the anxiety talking"

3. PRESENT MOMENT AWARENESS
Connect with the here and now through mindfulness.
The past causes depression. The future causes anxiety. The present is manageable.

4. SELF AS CONTEXT (Observer Self)
You are not your thoughts or feelings. You are the one observing them.
"I am not anxious — I am noticing anxiety."
This perspective gives stability during emotional storms.

5. VALUES CLARIFICATION
What matters most to you? What kind of person do you want to be?
Common values: family, learning, creativity, health, honesty, kindness, achievement
Values are different from goals — they are directions, not destinations.

6. COMMITTED ACTION
Take effective action guided by values, even in the presence of difficult thoughts/feelings.
Do what matters even when you don't feel like it.

=== ACT FOR COMMON ISSUES ===

ACADEMIC PRESSURE (India):
Values clarification: Is performing well about genuine learning, or only about parental approval?
Defusion: "I'm having the thought that failure means I'm worthless."
Acceptance: Allow exam anxiety without fighting it.

RELATIONSHIP PROBLEMS:
Values: What kind of son/daughter/partner/friend do you want to be?
Committed action: Act from values, not from fear or anger.

DEPRESSION:
The ACT model of depression: Experiential avoidance — avoiding feelings makes them worse.
Acceptance: Allow sadness without judgment.
Values: What small action today aligns with your values?

=== GENERAL MENTAL HEALTH KNOWLEDGE ===

ANXIETY — UNDERSTANDING IT:
Anxiety is the body's alarm system. It evolved to protect us from danger.
Symptoms: racing heart, tight chest, shallow breathing, racing thoughts, muscle tension.
The anxiety response is NOT dangerous — it feels awful but it is not harmful.
Anxiety peaks and passes — it cannot maintain its peak indefinitely.

ANXIETY MANAGEMENT:
1. Box breathing: 4 counts in, 4 hold, 4 out, 4 hold. Repeat 4 times.
2. 5-4-3-2-1 grounding: Name 5 things you see, 4 you hear, 3 you can touch, 2 you smell, 1 you taste.
3. Progressive muscle relaxation
4. Cold water on wrists or face
5. Physical exercise — burns off adrenaline

DEPRESSION — UNDERSTANDING IT:
Depression is not weakness or laziness. It is a medical condition affecting brain chemistry.
Symptoms: low mood, loss of interest, fatigue, sleep changes, appetite changes, difficulty concentrating.
Depression lies — it tells you nothing will help, nothing matters, you are worthless. These are symptoms, not facts.

DEPRESSION MANAGEMENT:
1. Behavioral activation — do one small activity even when you don't want to
2. Maintain routine — sleep, meals, movement
3. Social connection — isolation worsens depression
4. Sunlight and exercise — clinically proven to help
5. Reach out — talking helps

SLEEP HYGIENE:
Poor sleep worsens anxiety, depression, and stress significantly.
- Consistent sleep/wake time even on weekends
- No screens 1 hour before bed
- Cool, dark room
- No caffeine after 2pm
- Wind-down routine: reading, stretching, deep breathing

STRESS MANAGEMENT:
Physical: Exercise, sleep, nutrition, limiting caffeine
Cognitive: CBT techniques, reframing, problem-solving
Behavioral: Time management, saying no, breaks
Social: Talking to someone trusted
Spiritual/Meaning: Connection to values and purpose

SELF-COMPASSION (Dr. Kristin Neff):
Three components:
1. Self-kindness: Treat yourself as you would a good friend
2. Common humanity: Suffering is part of the shared human experience
3. Mindfulness: Hold painful thoughts in balanced awareness
The self-compassion break: "This is a moment of suffering. Suffering is part of life. May I be kind to myself."

INDIAN MENTAL HEALTH CONTEXT:
- Mental health stigma is real but changing
- Seeking help is a sign of strength, not weakness
- Family involvement can be both a stressor and a resource
- Academic and career pressure is a genuine source of distress
- Financial stress affects mental health significantly
- Religious and spiritual beliefs can be protective factors
- Community and belonging are important wellbeing factors
