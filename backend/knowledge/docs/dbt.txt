DIALECTICAL BEHAVIOR THERAPY (DBT) — CORE KNOWLEDGE BASE

=== WHAT IS DBT ===
Dialectical Behavior Therapy was developed by Dr. Marsha Linehan. It combines CBT with acceptance strategies from mindfulness and Eastern philosophy. The central dialectic is: accepting yourself as you are RIGHT NOW, while also working to change. DBT is especially effective for emotional dysregulation, intense emotions, self-harm urges, and relationship difficulties.

=== FOUR CORE SKILL MODULES ===

MODULE 1: MINDFULNESS
The foundation of all DBT skills. Being present in the moment without judgment.

WISE MIND: The balance between emotional mind and rational mind.
- Emotional Mind: Ruled entirely by feelings
- Rational Mind: Ruled entirely by logic and facts
- Wise Mind: The integration — acknowledges feelings AND uses reason

HOW TO PRACTICE:
- Observe: Notice thoughts and feelings without acting on them
- Describe: Put words to experiences without judgment
- Participate: Fully engage in the present moment
- Non-judgmentally: Drop evaluations like "good/bad", "should/shouldn't"
- One-mindfully: Focus on one thing at a time
- Effectively: Do what works, not what feels right

MODULE 2: DISTRESS TOLERANCE
Skills for surviving crisis moments without making things worse.

TIPP SKILLS (for intense emotions):
- Temperature: Splash cold water on face to calm nervous system instantly
- Intense exercise: 20 minutes of vigorous exercise burns off stress hormones
- Paced breathing: Slow exhale longer than inhale activates parasympathetic system
- Progressive relaxation: Systematically relax muscle groups

ACCEPTS (distract from crisis):
- Activities: Engage in a hobby or task
- Contributing: Help someone else
- Comparisons: Remember harder times you survived
- Emotions: Watch a movie, listen to music to shift emotion
- Pushing away: Mentally set the problem aside temporarily
- Thoughts: Read, do puzzles, count backwards
- Sensations: Cold shower, spicy food, strong sensation

IMPROVE THE MOMENT:
- Imagery: Visualize a safe, peaceful place
- Meaning: Find purpose in the suffering
- Prayer: Connect to something larger than yourself
- Relaxation: Progressive muscle relaxation
- One thing at a time: Focus only on this moment
- Vacation: Take a brief mental or physical break
- Encouragement: Talk kindly to yourself

PROS AND CONS: List benefits and costs of tolerating distress vs acting impulsively

MODULE 3: EMOTION REGULATION
Skills for understanding and changing emotional experiences.

UNDERSTANDING EMOTIONS:
- Emotions are not good or bad — they carry information
- Emotions have triggers, body sensations, action urges, and aftereffects
- Suppressing emotions intensifies them — acceptance reduces them

PLEASE SKILLS (reduce emotional vulnerability):
- Physical illness: Treat health problems
- Eating: Eat balanced, regular meals
- Avoid mood-altering substances
- Sleep: Regular sleep schedule
- Exercise: Regular physical activity

OPPOSITE ACTION:
When an emotion is not justified or effective, act opposite to the urge.
- Depression urge (withdraw) → Get active, reach out to people
- Anxiety urge (avoid) → Approach the feared situation gradually
- Anger urge (attack) → Be gentle, avoid the person temporarily
- Shame urge (hide) → Share with a safe person, hold head up

CHECKING THE FACTS:
- Identify the emotion and intensity
- What is the prompting event?
- What are the interpretations? Are they facts?
- Does the emotion fit the facts?

MODULE 4: INTERPERSONAL EFFECTIVENESS
Skills for navigating relationships and asking for what you need.

DEAR MAN (getting what you want):
- Describe: State the facts of the situation
- Express: Share your feelings using "I" statements
- Assert: Ask clearly for what you want
- Reinforce: Explain the positive outcome of getting it
- Mindful: Stay focused on your goal
- Appear confident: Use calm, direct body language
- Negotiate: Be willing to compromise

GIVE (maintaining relationships):
- Gentle: No attacks, threats, or judgment
- Interested: Listen and appear interested
- Validate: Acknowledge the other person's feelings
- Easy manner: Use humor, be light when appropriate

FAST (maintaining self-respect):
- Fair: Be fair to yourself AND the other person
- Apologies: Don't apologize excessively
- Stick to values: Don't compromise your values for approval
- Truthful: Don't lie or exaggerate

=== DBT FOR INDIAN CONTEXT ===

FAMILY ENMESHMENT:
Many Indian families have high emotional enmeshment. DBT skills help:
- Validate family's concern while asserting personal needs
- DEAR MAN for having difficult conversations with parents
- Wise Mind to balance family loyalty with personal needs

EMOTIONAL SUPPRESSION:
"Don't cry", "Be strong", "Don't share problems outside family" — common messages.
DBT approach: Emotions are valid information, not weakness. Naming emotions reduces their intensity.

SHAME AND MENTAL HEALTH STIGMA:
DBT opposite action: Instead of hiding struggles (shame urge), share with one safe person.
FAST skill: Your mental health needs are valid — don't apologize for having them.

=== CRISIS SURVIVAL ===
When in crisis (wanting to self-harm or harm others):
1. Use TIPP first — cold water on face immediately
2. Call a trusted person
3. Call a helpline: iCall 9152987821
4. Ride the wave — emotions peak and pass like waves
5. Do NOT make permanent decisions during temporary emotional storms
